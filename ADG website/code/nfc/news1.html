NFC Update Moves Tech Outside the Wallet

The NFC Forum this week unveiled an update to the near-field communication specification that will allow devices to communicate in a peer-to-peer fashion, actively exchanging data simultaneously.
Current implementations of NFC technology use an active reader and a passive device, in the case of an NFC tag or even Google Wallet, where the credit card information stored securely is asked for by the payment terminal, then acted upon.
Devices equipped with the Simple NDEF Exchange Protocol (SNEP), which the Forum is making available at no charge, could be sued to actively transmit data back and forth. As an example, Debbie Arnold, director of the NFC Forum, suggested that a photo taken with an NFC-equipped camera or smartphone could be touched to an NFC-equipped HDTV for playback.
In another, NFC could replace the need to "bump" phones together to exchange business contacts. But in a world where Wi-Fi and Bluetooth exist as wireless mediums for data, do we really need another mechanism? Arnold said that we do. "Have you ever tried to synchronize your Bluetooth headset, or your car, with your phone? It's not intuitive," she said. "With NFC, it's just touch and go."

NFC won't necessarily be actually used for the data transfer, either, Arnold said, meaning that users won't have to hold the phone next to another device to complete the data transaction. Instead, the NFC transaction will facilitate the data transaction, then get out of the way and let either Bluetooth or Wi-Fi take over, she said.
In the scenarios Arnold envisioned, however, the NFC antenna would be almost constantly on and enabled. "Real security is going to happen at an application layer, so if you're putting in transit tickets, or you're putting in credit card information, that's going to be encrypted, that's going to be securely downloaded," Arnold said. "We have a level of technical security in our specs. Beyond that, it's up to the individual.
"So today, if you have a transaction of less than $25 dollars on your contactless payment card, you may not require a PIN; if they're bigger than that you do put in a PIN. It'll be the same thing for your phone; some banks may say you don't get into your payment application at all until you type in a password. But the technology ins't controlling that; it's a combination of the technology, the application, and the individual."
